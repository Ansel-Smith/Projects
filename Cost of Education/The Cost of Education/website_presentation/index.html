The Cost of Education

A Summary of Financial Transactions of Enslavement at the University of Alabama

Ansel Smitha
aThe University of Alabama
Unpublished, September 8, 2020

Abstract

A study of the history of enslavement at the University of Alabama offers critical insight into the social and financial implications of enslavement in Alabama and its broader legacy in higher education in the United States.

The purpose of this project was to digitize financial transactions dating between 1828 and 1864 and develop descriptive statistics as a baseline for future historical inquiry.

Data was collected from the Boole Special Collections of the University of Alabama between August and November of 2024. Transactions were parsed and organized into a tidy dataset and key financial trends and relationships between social agents were visualized with the R Tidyverse.

Among the 239 unique instances analyzed, a total of $535,135.43, adjusted for inflation, not accounting for overlap, was found to be spent by the university for the purchase, hire, and board of enslaved individuals. With this baseline investigation, future statistical and social network analysis is possible to answer deeper questions about the university's history of enslavement and its contemporary financial impact.

Methods

  • Sourced documents from Hoole Special Collections and Digital Collections
  • Digitized and sorted documents into tidy dataset with Excel and R Tidyverse
  • Created plots and tables in R Plotly and GTables
  • Used Gephi to conduct social network analysis
  • Adjusted financial amounts for inflation using CPI estimates from Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis
